# Attentional Multidomain Feature Fusion for Underwater Acoustic Signal Classification
**IEEE JOURNAL OF OCEANIC ENGINEERING, VOL. 51, NO. 1, JANUARY 2026**
Yongchun Miao, Yingsong Li, Senior Member, IEEE, and Yuriy Zakharov, Senior Member, IEEE

## Abstract
Fusion of features from the time-domain, frequency-domain, and time–frequency (TF) domain significantly boosts the robustness of classification methods by providing a more comprehensive data representation. However, this fusion process can lead to challenges in maintaining distinct features from different domains, causing a loss of important information. To address these challenges, a novel attentional multidomain feature fusion network (AMFFNet) is introduced, which integrates features from the time, frequency, and TF domains using a three-branch feature fusion (TBFF) mechanism powered by attentional cross-correlation. A key component of this network is the adaptive personalized feature selection, which uses deep shapley additive explanations to identify and prioritize features across different domains, addressing the issue of irrelevant information overload. The TBFF enhances feature mapping by focusing on the most contributive features, ensuring that the network captures critical information while minimizing noise. By combining these features, the AMFFNet with the TBFF provides a comprehensive representation of the input multidomain features, preserving the most relevant aspects from each domain without significant loss of information. Comparative experiments on three curated public underwater acoustic signal data sets, marine mammal sound, ShipsEar, and combined underwater acoustic, covering six categories of signal sources (whale, dolphin, fish, seal, sea lion, and ship), demonstrate that the proposed method achieves robust classification performance and outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

**Index Terms**: Attentional multidomain feature fusion, cross-correlation feature, deep SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP), feature selection, underwater acoustic (UWA) signal classification.

## I. INTRODUCTION
Passive acoustic monitoring has emerged as a vital tool for observing a variety of underwater targets, including cetacean species. Underwater acoustic (UWA) signals received in the monitoring process come from diverse sound sources, including marine animals (whales, dolphins, fish, and seals), human activities (large ships and small boats), and physical processes (wind, waves, rain, and hurricanes). A primary goal of UWA signal classification is to reveal the types of information these signals can provide, aiding in our understanding and protection of the ocean and its inhabitants.

A typical procedure for signal classification involves the following steps: a) preprocessing UWA signals to remove invalid segments and perform data augmentation, b) extracting features from the preprocessed signals, and c) implementing a classifier to identify the signal classes. Time–frequency (TF) features have been shown to enhance the discrimination of UWA signals, providing reliable TF representations (TFRs) for signal classification. Our previous study demonstrated that a TF feature network (TFFNet), which uses features generated by the anisotropic chirplet transform (ACT), achieves high classification accuracy for UWA signals by preserving the rich contextual information of the TF structures. However, TFFNet struggles to accurately classify signals with TF features that resemble ocean background noise, which lack distinct structural information. These features indicate that TFRs of ship signals are not easily distinguishable from those of UWA background noise, yet their frequency features can still be discriminated. Thus, extracting diverse features from the signals may improve the performance of UWA signal classification.

Given the variability of useful features in signal classification, multifeature fusion (MFF) methods have been developed, including late fusion, early fusion, and space fusion. Late fusion involves creating several independent classifiers using different features. However, for UWA signal classification, this approach might not fully utilize the advantages of combining multiple features, as it may lead to minimal gains from a single classifier. Early fusion merges data at the feature level by integrating outputs from various feature extraction methods, but this can result in high-dimensional, redundant, and irrelevant features that reduce classification accuracy and increase computational cost.

Space fusion attempts to incorporate the flexibility of late fusion while simplifying the complexity typically involved in early fusion. Recent developments in space fusion, such as multiscale attention networks, spatial feature fusion based on a convolutional neural network (CNN), optimized learning models for MFF, and convolutional attention multibranch networks, aim to reduce the bias associated with individual features. However, these methods are mostly designed for fusion of 2-D features, like images or TF features.

A multifeature ensemble learning network using a 1-D CNN allows for the fusion of various types and quantities of features without restriction. This network performs deep feature mapping for each feature in parallel but fails to consider correlation between features. To better utilize features from the time, frequency, and TF domains, a MFF approach has been developed. This method uses feature selection techniques and Dempster–Shafer evidence theory to create high-quality feature sets. However, in deep networks, simple combining multiple features is insufficient for effective discriminative feature extraction. As a result, attentional feature fusion (AFF) has been introduced to emphasize informative local and global features. Building on this advantage, we aim to use attention feature fusion to enhance the signal classification using features from the time, frequency, and TF domains, making feature fusion more effective.

In this work, we introduce a novel attentional multidomain feature fusion network (AMFFNet) to improve UWA signal classification by exploring the complementary relationships between features from different domains, such as time, frequency, and TF. The proposed AMFFNet includes feature extraction, feature selection, three backbone CNNs, and three-branch feature fusion (TBFF) using attentional cross-correlation (ACC). To evaluate the classification performance of AMFFNet, experiments are conducted on three UWA signal data sets: Marine mammal sound (MMS), ShipsEar, and combined underwater acoustic (CUWA). The data sets include signal recordings from six categories of signal sources: whale, dolphin, fish, seal, sea lion, and ship, where the ship category comprises six subtypes: dredger, fish boat, motorboat, mussel boat, ocean liner, and sail boat.

The rest of this article is organized as follows. Section II describes the proposed AMFFNet in detail. Section III presents the experimental setup and results for the UWA signal data sets. Finally, Section IV concludes this article with a summary.

## II. PROPOSED METHOD
### A. Feature Extraction
To obtain high-discrimination features from UWA signals, it is essential to use various feature extraction methods across the time, frequency, and TF domains. These methods capture unique signal characteristics crucial for accurate classification and analysis.

- **Time-domain methods**: Six time-domain features are employed to characterize the morphological and statistical properties of the signals. Autoregressive coefficients capture short-term signal dependencies and temporal dynamics. Kurtosis and skewness describe the sharpness and asymmetry of waveforms, which are often indicative of impulsive or non-Gaussian components typical in underwater sound events. Permutation entropy (PENT) quantifies the complexity and randomness of the signal, while root mean square (rms) reflects the average signal power. Zero-crossing rate (ZCR) represents the rate of sign changes in the waveform, indicating roughness or high-frequency content. These features are selected to describe transient behaviors and statistical irregularities observed in marine mammal calls and ship-generated noise.
- **Frequency-domain methods**: After transforming the signal using the Fourier transform, seven frequency-domain features are extracted to represent spectral structure and perceptual characteristics. Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients are used to describe pitch and timbre in biological acoustics. Spectral bandwidth (SBW) and spectral rolloff (SRO) measure the spread and concentration of spectral energy, respectively, while spectral contrast (SC) captures differences between spectral peaks and valleys, useful for distinguishing tonal patterns. Spectral entropy and spectral flatness (SF) assess the degree of disorder and tonality in the spectrum. The chromagram (CG) encodes harmonic content and periodicity. These features are effective in distinguishing structured biological signals from unstructured anthropogenic noise.
- **TF-domain methods**: TF-domain features are derived using short-time Fourier transform (STFT), Wigner–Ville distribution (WVD), and ACT. Although STFT, WVD, and ACT all provide TFR of signals, and they extract similar TF components in theory, they differ in TR resolution, energy concentration, and adaptability to nonstationary structures, especially for fast-varying instantaneous frequency components found in UWA signals. STFT provides baseline representations for a wide range of signals, but suffers from fixed resolution. WVD offers high resolution and energy concentration but is affected by cross-term interference. ACT matches fast-varying components in UWA signals, producing more concentrated TFRs. Three TF analysis methods are considered to validate the discriminative capability of the proposed adaptive personalized feature selection (APFS) method for selecting the most suitable TFR in UWA signal classification.

Features extracted from the time, frequency, and TF domains enhance signal discrimination by capturing the unique physical and perceptual characteristics of different UWA signals. However, not all features are essential for classifying specific categories. We use Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient to analyze the relationships between the extracted features, as demonstrated in the correlation matrix heatmaps of whale and ship signals. These results indicate the presence of redundant or irrelevant features, which can lead to overfitting in classification models. Thus, selecting a small subset of features that reduces redundancy and increases relevance to each category is crucial.

### B. Adaptive Personalized Feature Selection
To select discriminative features for UWA signals and reduce overfitting in classification models, we introduce the APFS method. APFS aggregates the features extracted in the preceding subsection from the time \(Z^{t}\) , frequency \(Z^{f}\) , and TF \(Z^{t f}\) domains into a full feature pool:
\[Z_{org }=Z^{t} \cup Z^{f} \cup Z^{t f}=\left\{z_{1}, ..., z_{M_{f}}\right\}\]
where \(M_{f}\) is the total number of features.

The Shapley value for feature m is its contribution to the prediction, weighted over all possible feature combinations:
\[\psi_{m}=\sum_{A \subseteq\left\{1, ..., M_{f}\right\} \backslash\{m\}} \frac{|A| !\left(M_{f}-|A|-1\right) !}{M_{f} !} \left[R(A \cup m)-R(A)\right]\]
where A is a subset of features of cardinality \(|A|\) , \(R(A \cup m)\) is the contribution to prediction including feature m ,and \(R(A)\) excluding it. Shapley values measure each feature’s impact on predictions, but calculating them is computationally expensive, requiring \(O(2^{M_{f}})\) operations due to the exponential number of feature combinations.

SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) is a widely used method for explaining both global and local model predictions by calculating Shapley values \(\psi_{m}\) . SHAP defines these Shapley value explanations as:
\[g\left(p'\right)=\psi_{0}+\sum_{m=1}^{M_{f}} \psi_{m} p_{m}'\]
where \(\psi_{0} \in \mathbb{R}\) is the average model output, \(p' \in{0,1}_{f}^{M}\) is the coalition vector indicating the presence of a feature when the corresponding element \(p_{m}'\) of \(p'\) is 1, and its absence when it is 0.

While SHAP effectively provides stable importance scores for global features and individual predictions, it does not include an algorithm for ranking and selecting the top most important features. To overcome this, methods like Boruta SHAP and deep SHAP have been developed to identify a subset of important features and provide a consistent ranking of global features for better model explanation. Inspired by Boruta SHAP and deep SHAP, the APFS method is created to address the challenge of selecting heterogeneous features. APFS combines the robust feature selection of Boruta SHAP with the interpretability of deep SHAP, allowing for the selection of relevant features across diverse data sets.

In the APFS algorithm, a signal data set, such as the UWA signal data set, is used for feature selection, focusing on the importance of individual features for signal classification. Given the diverse nature of features across domains, three backbone CNNs are used to train a multidomain CNN classifier: 1-D CNNs for \(Z^{t}\) and \(Z^{f}\) , and a 2-D CNN (e.g., VGG, ResNet, MobileNet) applied to each TF map in \(Z^{t f}\) . To enhance interpretability, the Z -score for SHAP values is used, which allows for comparing the impact of features across different scales. The Z -score of features is calculated as: \(Z_{score }=(g(p')-\mu) / \delta\) , where μ is the mean, and δ is the variance of the accuracy loss.

For each feature in \(Z_{hist }^{i}\) , two one-tailed tests are conducted to assess the statistical significance of feature performance against a baseline success rate of 0.5. If there are M important features among the \(M_{f}\) total features and the success rate \(p_{0}\) exceeds 0.5, the acceptance probability \(P_{apt }^{i}\) is calculated. Similarly, the rejection probability \(P_{rjt}^{i}\) is calculated if we are testing whether \(p_{0}<0.5\) by summing over the range from M to \(M_{f}\) . The set of important features \(Z_{ap1 }^{i}\) and unimportant features \(Z_{rjt}^{i}\) is then determined based on the acceptance and rejection probabilities.

Feature importance evaluation results using Z -Score for whale and ship signals with the proposed APFS method after \(N=100\) iterations show that different sets of features are critical for whale and ship signal classification. Given that some important features still exhibit correlation, ZCR and PENT are chosen from the time domain, CG and SBW from the frequency domain, and ACT from the TF domain.

### C. Three Backbone CNNs
The selected important features consist of 1-D time-domain and frequency-domain features, as well as 2-D TF features. To extract deep features from each domain while maintaining the integrity of the information, three backbone CNNs are designed for these three domains.

Given a sequence of signals \(X={x_{1}, x_{2}, ..., x_{L}}\) , where L is the number of signals, important features are extracted from each signal \(x_{1}\) across three domains: time, frequency, and TF. These features are denoted as \(F_{l}^{t}=[f_{1}^{t} ; ... ; f_{N_{t}}^{t}]\) , \(F_{l}^{f}=[f_{1}^{f} ; ... ; f_{N_{f}}^{f}]\) and \(S_{l}=[f_{1}^{t f} ; ... ; f_{N_{S}}^{t f}]\) , where \(N_{t}\) . \(N_{f}\) ,and \(N_{S}\) are the number of selected important features in the time, frequency, and TF domains, respectively. Based on the above feature selection results, \(N_{t}=2\) , \(N_{f}=2\) ,and \(N_{S}=1\) . The sets of features across all signals are represented as \(F^{t}=[F_{1}^{t}, F_{2}^{t}, ..., F_{L}^{t}]\) for time-domain features, \(F^{f}=[F_{1}^{f}, F_{2}^{f}, ..., F_{L}^{f}]\) for frequency-domain features, and \(S=[S_{1}, S_{2}, ..., S_{L}]\) for TF features.

To enhance the network’s flexibility in adding and removing important features within each domain, each backbone network is designed to extract deep features from the corresponding important features. For features in the time and frequency domains, generic long short-term memory (LSTM) and 1-D CNN backbone networks can be used to match the structure of these input features. For features in the TF domain, backbone networks like VGG, ResNet, and the MobileNet series are suitable due to their compatibility with various network architectures and strong performance in deep feature representation. A total of \(N_{t}+N_{f}+N_{S}\) backbone networks operate in parallel to extract deep features from their respective domains. These deep features are then aggregated within each domain using an attention accumulation layer, producing a deep feature map for that domain. The resulting deep features from the three domains are represented as \(\overline{F}_{k}^{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 ×n_{t}}\) , \(\overline{F}_{k}^{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 ×n_{f}}\) , and \(\overline{S}_{k} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×n_{t}}\) , where \(n_{t}\) and \(n_{f}\) are the time and frequency dimensions of the features, respectively, and \(k=1,2, ..., K\) , with K being the number of channels.

### D. Three-Branch Feature Fusion
Different from concatenation or addition operations in the fusion layer, TBFF involves cross-correlation of deep features and attention-based fusion to dynamically weigh features based on their relevance to the classification task. In TBFF, the time-domain and frequency-domain features are first cross-correlated with the TF domain features to optimize each feature set for fusion. This process leverages the unique insights provided by each domain and aims to create a more comprehensive understanding of the signal properties, which cannot be achieved when each domain is analyzed independently.

The cross-correlation of deep features, \(\overline{F}^{t}\) \(\overline{F}^{f}\) and \(\bar{S}\) , is computed to capture the relevance across the features. The cross-correlation between \(\overline{F}_{k}^{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 ×n_{t}}\) and \(\tilde{S}_{k} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×n_{t}}\) for the kth features in \(\overline{F}^{t}\) and \(\bar{S}\) is calculated as:
\[S_{k}^{t-t f}=\overline{S}_{k} w_{k}^{t} \overline{F}_{k}^{t}\]
where \(S_{k}^{t-t f} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×n_{t}}\) and \(w_{k}^{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{t} ×1}\) are the cross-correlation weights. Similarly, the cross-correlation between \(\overline{F}_{k}^{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{1 ×n_{f}}\) and \(S_{k}\) is given by:
\[S_{k}^{f \cdot t f}=\overline{S}_{k}^{T} w_{k}^{f} \overline{F}_{k}^{f}\]
where \(S_{k}^{f \cdot t f} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{t} ×n_{f}}\) . \(w_{k}^{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×1}\) .and \(\tilde{S}_{k}^{T}\) is the transpose of \(S_{k}\)

The matrices \(S_{k}^{t-t f}\) and \(S_{k}^{f \cdot t f}\) measure the correlations between time-domain and TF-domain features, and between frequency-domain and TF-domain features, respectively. High correlation coefficients in these matrices indicate strong relationships between the corresponding domain features. This method can utilize the complementary nature of time-domain, frequency-domain, and TF-domain features, thereby enhancing the network’s performance.

While correlation matrices can provide insights into the relationships between features, the joint correlation matrix tends to weaken the representation of time and frequency features compared to TF features. This discrepancy can lead to potential information loss or redundancy, which may affect the accuracy and reliability of the classification model. Attention mechanisms can dynamically select relevant features and reduce the impact of irrelevant or redundant data. The attentional cross-correlation (ACC) maps \(C_{k}^{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×n_{t}}\) for time-domain features are defined by:
\[C_{k}^{t}=\sigma\left(B\left(w_{c 1}^{t} \overline{F}_{k}^{t}+w_{c 2}^{t} S_{k}^{t-t f}\right)\right)\]
where \(\sigma(\cdot)\) represents the nonlinear activation function, the Gaussian error linear unit, and \(B(-)\) denotes batch normalization (BN). The learnable weight matrices \(w_{c 1}^{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×1}\) and \(w_{c 2}^{t} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×n_{f}}\) correspond to layers in the time-attention branch. Similarly, the attention maps \(C_{k}^{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{f} ×n_{t}}\) for frequency-domain features are defined by:
\[C_{k}^{f}=\sigma \left( B\left( \left[ w_{c 1}^{f} \overline {F}_{k}^{f}+w_{c 2}^{f}S_{k}^{f-tf}\right] ^{T}\right) \right)\]
where the learnable weight matrices \(w_{c 1}^{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{t} ×1}\) and \(w_{c 2}^{f} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_{t} ×n_{t}}\) are associated with the layers in the frequency-attention branch.

In the fusion layer, combining multiple features from two branches can improve the accuracy of signal classification. This is achieved through late fusion, where content vectors from \(C_{k}^{t}\) , \(C_{k}^{f}\) ,and \(S_{k}\) are concatenated into a single vector used for signal class prediction. While this method integrates various features, it assigns the same weight to each feature vector during fusion. However, the fusion layer lacks the ability to exploit multiple types of features because it does not allow the relative weights of each feature type to change based on the context of each signal class.

The proposed method extends the attention mechanism to TBFF. Attention-based feature fusion is performed using the following formula:
\[G_{k}=\sigma \left( B\left( w_{k}^{g_{t}}C_{k}^{t}+w_{k}^{g_{f}}C_{k}^{f}+w_{k}^{g_{s}}\overline {S}_{k}\right) \right)\]
where the attention weights \(w_{k}^{g_{t}}\) , \(w_{k}^{g_{f}}\) ,and \(w_{k}^{g_{s}}\) are calculated as:
\[w_{k}^{g_{t}}=\frac{e^{C_{k}^{t}}}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} e^{C_{n}^{t}}}, w_{k}^{g_{f}}=\frac{e^{C_{k}^{f}}}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} e^{C_{n}^{f}}}, w_{k}^{g_{s}}=\frac{e^{\overline{S}_{k}}}{\sum_{n=1}^{N} e^{\overline{S}_{n}}}\]
using softmax functions over their respective feature activations. These weights reflect the relative importance of the time, frequency, and TF domain features. This mechanism allows the network to suppress less informative features while emphasizing more discriminative ones, adapting to varying input conditions.

In the TBFF, the attention mechanism dynamically adjusts the contributions of each feature modality, enabling one domain to compensate for the potential weaknesses of the others. This dynamic weighting enhances robustness by ensuring that more informative domains (e.g., frequency domain for tonal ship sounds) are prioritized, while noisy or redundant features are attenuated.

### E. Classifier for UWA Signals
To make the final class prediction, the fused feature vector \(G_{k}\) is input into a classifier, and the probability of class c is computed using the softmax function:
\[P\left(y_{k}=y_{c} | G_{k}\right)=softmax\left(w_{k}^{g} G_{k}+b_{k}\right)\]
where \(w_{k}^{g}\) and \(b_{k}\) are the weight matrix and bias vector of the classifier, respectively.

Let the training data set be \(D={(x_{l}, y_{l})}_{l=1}^{L}\) , where \(l=1, ..., L\) . After applying the softmax function, each input has an associated probability \(P(y_{l} | G_{l})\) . Using the trained network and its parameters θ , the predicted class \(\hat{y}_{l}\) for input \(x_{i}\) is the one with the highest probability:
\[\hat{y}_{l}\left(x_{l}, \theta\right)=arg max _{c}\left\{P\left(y_{l}=y_{c} | G_{l}\right)\right\} .\]

To penalize the differences between predictions and actual targets y , a loss function \(L(\theta)\) is defined using cross-entropy loss, generalized for multiple classes:
\[\mathcal{L}(\theta)=-\frac{1}{L} \sum_{l=1}^{L} y_{l} log \left(\hat{y}_{l}\left(x_{l}, \theta\right)\right)+\frac{\lambda}{2}\| \theta\| _{2}^{2}\]
where the \(\ell_{2}\) -norm regularization term mitigates overfitting by penalizing large parameter values, with the hyperparameter λ controlling its influence relative to the data fitting term.

## III. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
### A. UWA Data Sets and Settings
To validate the effectiveness of the proposed AMFFNet, we collected signals from major categories of marine biological sounds and publicly available ship signals, forming three distinct data sets:
1. **MMS data set**: Consists of audio recordings categorized into five classes: whale, dolphin, fish, seal, and sea lion. Sourced from Discovery of Sound in the Sea, Watkin’s MMS database, Voices in the Sea, Whale FM, and web-crawled files.
2. **ShipsEar**: Includes 90 audio recordings from 11 different types of vessels; a subset of six categories is selected: dredger, fish boat, motorboat, mussel boat, ocean liner, and sailboat.
3. **CUWA data set**: Merged by MMS and ShipsEar, organized into six major categories (CUWA6: whale, fish, seal, sea lion, dolphin, ship) and 11 subclasses (CUWA11).

### B. Data Augmentation
To diversify the training set, several data augmentation techniques are applied:
- Time stretching (TS): factors of 0.85, 0.97, 1.09, 1.21.
- Small-scale pitch shifting (SPS): −1, -0.5, 0.5, 1 semitone.
- Large-scale pitch shifting (LPS): -3, -2, 2, 3 semitone.
- Dynamic range compression (DRC): four parameter settings (Dolby E standard for music, film, speech; Icecast radio standard).
- Ambient noise (AN) mixing: weighted combination with wave/wind/rain noise, weight 0.1–0.5.

Analysis shows pitch augmentations have consistent positive impact; DRC and AN negatively affect ship class classification due to masking. Class-conditional augmentation can further improve performance.

### C. Implementation Details and Evaluation Metrics
- Signal processing: Align/center signals, time stamps=12288; standardize to zero mean/unit variance; frequency/time sampling points=1024.
- Data split: 70% training, 20% validation, 10% testing.
- Implementation: Python+PyTorch, NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3060; initial lr=0.001, Adam optimizer, batch size=16, early stopping, 300 epochs, 10-epoch linear warm-up.
- Backbone: 1-D CNN+ResNet50 V2; selected features: ZCR, PENT, CG, SBW, ACT.
- Metrics: accuracy (A), precision (P), recall (R), average precision (AP), mean AP (mAP), F1-Score, Cohen’s Kappa coefficient (κ), ROC, AUC, confusion matrix.

## IV. CONCLUSION
A novel AMFFNet is developed that prioritizes feature combinations contributing to accurate predictions by capturing complementary relationships between different types of features. By integrating multiple features and focusing on the most relevant information, this network achieves high accuracy and efficiency in UWA signal classification. The APFS method uses deep SHAP to calculate feature importance and applies a time-shifted personalized model to identify all relevant features for specific categories within a UWA signal data set. By extracting features from the time, frequency, and TF domains and filtering them for relevance, APFS addresses the challenge of irrelevant information overload.

An ACC module enhances deep feature mapping across time, frequency, and TF domains by computing attention weights that focus on the most contributive features in each domain. A TBFF module, utilizing ACC, is designed to combine these contributive feature representations, which are then fed into fully connected layers for UWA signal classification. In the TBFF, deep features from the time and frequency domains are combined in parallel with features from the TF domain using ACC. The results of this initial fusion are further merged with TF domain features through another ACC, creating a comprehensive feature representation.

Experimental results on the MMS, ShipsEar, and CUWA data sets show that the proposed AMFFNet outperforms existing methods, achieving superior classification accuracy and robust performance across all tested scenarios. Nevertheless, future work will explore the remaining challenges in classifying signals from overlapping sound sources with concurrent activity.
